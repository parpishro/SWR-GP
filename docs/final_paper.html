Gaussian Process Kernel Regression for Hydrological Modeling

A Temporal Adaptation of the NN-GLS Framework

Author

Par Pishrobat

Published

March 1, 2026

Introduction

Hydrological time series forecasting underpins water resource planning, flood and drought preparedness, and scientific inference about watershed dynamics. In rainfall-runoff modeling, streamflow is the combined effect of several latent subprocesses (overland flow, baseflow), each responding to rainfall on its own delay scale. The Sliding Window Regression (SWR) model captures this structure by representing each subprocess through a kernelized lag profile and combining the resulting predictors linearly, producing a model that is both predictive and hydrologically interpretable (Schrunner et al. 2023).

However, residuals from SWR fits often retain substantial temporal dependence. When this dependence is handled only through the Cochrane–Orcutt autoregressive correction, the boundary between hydrological signal and dependence adjustment blurs. This paper takes a different route, adapting the NN-GLS framework of Zhan and Datta (Zhan and Datta 2024) from its original geostatistical setting to the temporal domain. The central principle is to preserve the interpretable SWR mean function while replacing ad hoc residual correction with a sparse temporal Gaussian process covariance model.

The separation between mean and residual dependence is the core contribution. Modeling autocorrelation in the covariance layer, rather than folding it back into the mean equation, can improve stability and probabilistic calibration without sacrificing interpretability. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 defines the autocorrelation problem, Section 3 introduces the geostatistical template, and Section 4 develops the temporal SWR-GP adaptation. Section 5 evaluates the method through simulation and real-data analysis, and Section 6 discusses limitations and further directions.

Autocorrelation Bottleneck in Hydrological Modeling

Let \(x_t\) and \(y_t\) denote rainfall and streamflow at time \(t\). In the SWR framework, each latent runoff component \(k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}\) is represented by a lag kernel \(\kappa_{k,\cdot}\) applied to the rainfall history, so that \[ X_t^{(k)} = \sum_{s=0}^{L} \kappa_{k,s} x_{t-s}, \qquad \widetilde{y}_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \beta_k X_t^{(k)}. \] The mean function \(\widetilde{y}_t\) therefore decomposes streamflow into a small number of delayed rainfall responses, each with its own weight and lag profile. In the Gaussian specification used here, the \(k\)-th kernel is centered at lag \(\delta_k\) with spread \(\sigma_k\), so the parameters retain a direct hydrological interpretation in terms of response timing and temporal dispersion.

The central difficulty arises after the mean structure has been fitted. Even when SWR explains a substantial share of the rainfall-runoff signal, the residual process retains marked temporal dependence. This is expected in hydrological series: storage effects, recession dynamics, omitted state variables, and unresolved heterogeneity all generate serially correlated departures from the mean equation. Ignoring that dependence distorts uncertainty quantification and effectively attributes part of the residual persistence to the mean structure.

The usual correction is to treat the fitted residuals \(\hat r_t = y_t - \hat{\widetilde y}_t\) as autoregressive and to model them as \[ \hat r_t = \sum_{j=1}^{p}\phi_j \hat r_{t-j} + u_t, \] where \(u_t\) is taken to be serially uncorrelated. In this formulation, the SWR mean parameters are estimated first, and the autoregressive coefficients are then estimated from the residual series. The order \(p\) is selected progressively over a bounded candidate set by requiring the fitted innovations to exhibit reduced serial dependence. The resulting one-step corrected predictor has the form \[ \hat y_t^{\mathrm{AR}} = \hat{\widetilde y}_t + \sum_{j=1}^{\hat p}\hat\phi_j\bigl(y_{t-j}-\hat{\widetilde y}_{t-j}\bigr), \qquad t>\hat p, \] with the same correction applied on the transformed scale when a response transformation is used.

This procedure is effective as a residual adjustment but does not change the underlying modeling architecture. Dependence is introduced only after the mean has been fitted, and the autoregressive order is chosen by a sequential diagnostic rule. The dependence parameters are tied to the regression correction rather than to an explicit covariance model, so the mean and dependence components remain only partially separated.

A Geostatistical Blueprint for Modeling Error Dependence

The Spatial Gaussian Process Model

The NN-GLS framework of Zhan and Datta provides the methodological foundation by embedding flexible mean estimators within a Gaussian process covariance model for spatially referenced data. This section summarizes the key elements of that framework.

Consider observations collected at spatial locations \(s_1,\ldots,s_n\). Let \(Y_i = Y(s_i)\) denote the response, let \(\mathbf{X}_i = \mathbf{X}(s_i) \in \mathbb{R}^d\) denote the covariate vector at location \(s_i\), and write \(\mathbf{Y} = (Y_1,\ldots,Y_n)^\top\). The data-generating process is specified as a Gaussian process with a nonlinear mean: \[ Y(s) = f(\mathbf{X}(s)) + \epsilon(s), \qquad \epsilon(\cdot) \sim \mathrm{GP}(0, \Sigma(\cdot,\cdot)), \tag{1}\] so that the joint likelihood at the observed locations takes the form \[ (\mathbf{Y} \mid \mathbf{X}) \sim \mathcal{N}\bigl(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{X}),\, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}\bigr), \tag{2}\] where \(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{X}) = (f(\mathbf{X}_1),\ldots,f(\mathbf{X}_n))^\top\) and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) is the \(n \times n\) covariance matrix parameterized by a low-dimensional vector \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\). This formulation separates the covariate effect (modeled through \(f\)) from the spatial dependence (modeled through \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\)). The separation allows the mean to be estimated without conflating signal with spatial structure, and it enables kriging at new locations without sacrificing interpretability.

Generalized Least Squares and Decorrelation

When the error process is spatially correlated, estimating \(f\) by minimizing the ordinary least squares (OLS) loss \(\sum_i (Y_i - f(\mathbf{X}_i))^2\) ignores the dependence and leads to inefficient estimates. Zhan and Datta instead propose minimizing the generalized least squares (GLS) loss \[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GLS}}(f) = \frac{1}{n}\bigl(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{X})\bigr)^\top \mathbf{Q} \bigl(\mathbf{Y} - \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{X})\bigr), \tag{3}\] where \(\mathbf{Q}\) is a working precision matrix, ideally equal to \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\). When \(\mathbf{Q} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\), minimizing Equation 3 is equivalent to maximum likelihood estimation of \(f\) under the Gaussian process model Equation 2.

A central observation is that any GLS loss admits an equivalent OLS representation on decorrelated data. Let \(\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{A}^\top\mathbf{A}\), where \(\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}^{1/2}\), denote a Cholesky factorization of the precision. Defining \(\mathbf{Y}^* = \mathbf{Q}^{1/2}\mathbf{Y}\) and \(\mathbf{f}^*(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbf{Q}^{1/2}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{X})\), the GLS loss becomes \[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GLS}}(f) = \frac{1}{n}\bigl(\mathbf{Y}^* - \mathbf{f}^*(\mathbf{X})\bigr)^\top \bigl(\mathbf{Y}^* - \mathbf{f}^*(\mathbf{X})\bigr). \tag{4}\] This reformulation reduces the estimation problem to OLS on transformed data while retaining the efficiency of GLS.

Nearest-Neighbor Gaussian Process Approximation

Full Gaussian process inference requires \(O(n^3)\) time and \(O(n^2)\) storage for the dense covariance operations, rendering it infeasible for even moderately large datasets. To restore scalability, Zhan and Datta employ the Nearest-Neighbor Gaussian Process (NNGP) approximation (Datta et al. 2016), which replaces the dense precision matrix with a sparse conditional factorization.

The construction proceeds as follows. Given a directed acyclic graph (DAG) over the \(n\) locations, let \(N(i)\) denote the neighbor set of location \(s_i\), with \(|N(i)| \leq m \ll n\). For each index \(i\), the conditional distribution of \(Y_i\) given its neighbors \(\mathbf{Y}_{N(i)}\) under the full GP yields the kriging coefficients \[ \mathbf{b}_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(i),N(i)}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(i),i}, \tag{5}\] and the conditional variance \[ F_i = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{ii} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{i,N(i)} \mathbf{b}_i. \tag{6}\] Collecting the vectors \(\mathbf{b}_i\) into a sparse lower-triangular matrix \(\mathbf{B}\) (with \(B_{i,N(i)} = \mathbf{b}_i^\top\) and zero elsewhere) and the scalars \(F_i\) into the diagonal matrix \(\mathbf{F} = \operatorname{diag}(F_1,\ldots,F_n)\), the NNGP working precision takes the form \[ \mathbf{Q} = \widetilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{-1} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{B})^\top \mathbf{F}^{-1} (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{B}). \tag{7}\] Crucially, this precision matrix is never formed or stored as a dense \(n \times n\) object. Each row of \(\mathbf{B}\) has at most \(m\) nonzero entries, and \(\mathbf{F}\) is diagonal, so the construction requires only \(n\) local solves of dimension \(m \times m\), yielding \(O(nm^3)\) total cost. The decorrelation map is sequential: for each unit \(i\), \[ Y_i^* = F_i^{-1/2}\bigl(Y_i - \mathbf{b}_i^\top \mathbf{Y}_{N(i)}\bigr), \tag{8}\] which requires \(O(nm)\) total work. Sparsity thus enters through local conditional structure rather than through truncation of a dense covariance.

Neural Network Means under GLS: the NN-GLS Algorithm

A distinctive feature of the Zhan and Datta framework is that the mean function \(f\) in Equation 1 need not be restricted to a linear form. They propose modeling \(f\) with a feed-forward neural network (multi-layer perceptron) and estimating its parameters by minimizing the GLS loss Equation 3 rather than the standard OLS loss. This yields NN-GLS: a neural network whose training accounts for spatial dependence through the GP covariance.

Concretely, an \(L\)-layer MLP maps the input \(\mathbf{X}_i\) through successive hidden layers with weight matrices \(\mathbf{W}_{(l)}\) and nonlinear activation functions \(g_l(\cdot)\) to produce the output \(O_i = f(\mathbf{X}_i)\). In standard (non-spatial) neural network regression, the weights are estimated by backpropagation on the OLS loss \(\sum_i (Y_i - O_i)^2\). NN-GLS instead minimizes the GLS loss with the NNGP precision, which, by the decorrelation equivalence Equation 4, reduces to \[ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{GLS}}(f) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(Y_i^* - O_i^*\bigr)^2, \tag{9}\] where \(Y_i^* = \mathbf{v}_i^\top \mathbf{Y}_{N^*[i]}\) and \(O_i^* = \mathbf{v}_i^\top \mathbf{O}_{N^*[i]}\) are decorrelated responses and outputs, respectively, \(N^*[i] = \{i\} \cup N(i)\) is the inclusive neighborhood, and the graph convolution weights are \[ \mathbf{v}_i^\top = F_i^{-1/2}\bigl(1, -\mathbf{b}_i^\top\bigr). \tag{10}\] This representation reveals NN-GLS as a graph neural network (GNN) on the nearest-neighbor DAG. The feed-forward MLP maps covariates to a predicted mean at each node, and two graph-convolution layers decorrelate both output and response using kriging weights before OLS comparison (Zhan and Datta 2024). Because the NNGP factors Equation 5–Equation 6 involve only \(m \times m\) local solves, the loss Equation 9 decomposes into a sum over data units, admits mini-batching, and supports standard backpropagation. The covariance parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) enter through the graph-convolution weights Equation 10 and are updated periodically by maximizing the NNGP log-likelihood \[ \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\Bigl[\bigl(Y_i^*(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - O_i^*(\boldsymbol{\theta})\bigr)^2 + \log F_i(\boldsymbol{\theta})\Bigr], \tag{11}\] alternating with gradient-descent updates of the network weights.

Kriging Predictions

Because NN-GLS retains the Gaussian process model structure, predictions at new locations follow directly from kriging. Given a new location \(s_0\) with covariates \(\mathbf{X}_0\), the MLP first produces the mean prediction \(\hat{f}(\mathbf{X}_0)\). The location \(s_0\) is then added to the NNGP DAG with neighbor set \(N(0)\), and the kriging predictor is \[ \hat{Y}_0 = \hat{f}(\mathbf{X}_0) + \boldsymbol{\Sigma}(s_0, N(0))\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}(N(0), N(0))^{-1}\bigl(\mathbf{Y}_{N(0)} - \hat{\mathbf{f}}_{N(0)}\bigr), \tag{12}\] with associated conditional variance \(\sigma_0^2 = F_{00}\). The kriging correction adds a residual adjustment based on observed departures at nearby locations, improving point predictions and providing a principled basis for prediction intervals. In the GNN representation, this amounts to deconvolving the decorrelated prediction layer over the DAG, so that kriging is performed entirely within the neural architecture.

Theoretical Guarantees

Zhan and Datta provide the first large-sample theoretical results for neural network estimators under spatially correlated errors from an irregular set of locations. Their main contributions are:

  1. Existence (Theorem 1 of (Zhan and Datta 2024)). Under assumptions on the boundedness of the covariance eigenvalues and the sieve construction of the function class, a minimizer of the GLS loss over the neural network sieve exists.

  2. Consistency (Theorem 2 of (Zhan and Datta 2024)). Under additionally the scaling condition on the sieve complexity, the NN-GLS estimator \(\hat{f}_n\) satisfies \(\|\hat{f}_n - f_0\|_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} 0\), where \(\|g\|_n^2 = n^{-1}\sum_i g^2(\mathbf{X}_i)\). The result holds for any continuous mean function \(f_0\) and accommodates irregularly observed locations in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) under increasing-domain asymptotics.

  3. Finite-sample error rate (Theorem 3 of (Zhan and Datta 2024)). The estimation error depends on the spectral interval of the discrepancy matrix \(\mathbf{E} = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{1/2}\mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{1/2}\). When \(\mathbf{Q}\) approximates \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1}\) well, \(\mathbf{E}\) is close to the identity and the error rate is near-optimal. This result directly motivates accurate covariance modeling: ignoring spatial dependence (using OLS, i.e., \(\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{I}\)) inflates the spectral interval and leads to larger estimation error.

These results justify NNGP-based GLS as a principled statistical procedure: the sparse precision approximation provides the asymptotic bridge between dense GP theory and scalable estimation, and the finite-sample bound quantifies the cost of covariance misspecification.

Temporal SWR-GP: Model Specification and Estimation

Model Formulation

Let \(y_t\) denote streamflow at time \(t \in \{1,\ldots,n\}\), let \(x_t\) denote rainfall, and let \(X_t^{(1)},\ldots,X_t^{(K)}\) denote the kernelized rainfall predictors defined in Section 2. Writing \(\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\beta_1,\ldots,\beta_K)^\top\) and \(\widetilde{y}_t = \sum_{k=1}^{K}\beta_k X_t^{(k)}\), the temporal model is \[ y_t = \widetilde{y}_t + \epsilon_t, \qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} = (\epsilon_1,\ldots,\epsilon_n)^\top \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0},\, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}(\boldsymbol{\theta})), \tag{13}\] where the mean function is the SWR linear predictor from Section 2 and the residual process is a zero-mean Gaussian process with temporal covariance \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\). The mean is linear in \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) for fixed kernel-shape parameters, a property exploited in the estimation procedure below.

Temporal Matérn Covariance

Residual dependence is modeled by a stationary Mat'{e}rn covariance (Matérn 1960) with nugget: \[ \Sigma_{tt'} = \sigma^2\, M_\nu\!\left(\frac{|t - t'|}{\phi}\right) + \tau^2 \mathbf{1}_{t = t'}, \tag{14}\] where \(\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\sigma^2, \phi, \nu, \tau^2)\): \(\sigma^2\) is the signal variance, \(\phi\) the temporal range controlling decorrelation rate, \(\nu\) the smoothness, and \(\tau^2\) the nugget variance (measurement noise or unresolved microscale variability). The Matérn function \(M_\nu(h)\) has closed-form expressions for half-integer \(\nu\); in the present implementation, \(\nu\) is fixed at \(1.5\), yielding \[ M_{3/2}(h) = \left(1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}\, h}{\phi}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}\, h}{\phi}\right). \tag{15}\] This choice provides a once-differentiable process, keeping the number of free covariance parameters at three (\(\sigma^2\), \(\phi\), \(\tau^2\)). The original NN-GLS framework fixes the exponential covariance (\(\nu = 0.5\)), which is standard for rough spatial fields. In the temporal setting, \(\nu = 1.5\) is more appropriate: daily streamflow residuals reflect aggregated storage and recession dynamics that are physically smooth on a daily timescale, unlike the nowhere-differentiable sample paths implied by \(\nu = 0.5\). Estimating \(\nu\) continuously would require evaluating the modified Bessel function inside the NNGP inner loop, at substantial computational cost, while providing limited benefit given that the likelihood is dominated by the range and variance ratio. A discrete comparison over \(\nu \in \{0.5, 1.5, 2.5\}\) could test sensitivity, but is not pursued here.

Temporal NNGP Construction

Scalable inference uses an ordered-neighbor NNGP approximation that exploits the natural temporal ordering. For each time index \(t\), the neighbor set consists of the \(m\) most recent predecessors: \[ N(t) = \{t-1,\, t-2,\, \ldots,\, t - \min(t, m)\}. \tag{16}\] This is the temporal analogue of the spatial nearest-neighbor DAG, with two simplifying properties: the DAG ordering is given by time itself (no arbitrary ordering is needed), and the neighbor set is deterministic given \(m\). For each \(t\), the kriging coefficients and conditional variance are computed as in Equation 5–Equation 6: \[ \mathbf{b}_t = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(t),N(t)}^{-1}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(t),t}, \qquad F_t = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{tt} - \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{t,N(t)}\,\mathbf{b}_t, \tag{17}\] where \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(t),N(t)}\) is the \(|N(t)| \times |N(t)|\) submatrix of pairwise Matérn covariances among the neighbors (including nugget on the diagonal), and \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{N(t),t}\) is the cross-covariance vector. These are assembled into the sparse matrices \(\mathbf{B}\) and \(\mathbf{F}\) as in Equation 7; \(\mathbf{F}\) is stored as a length-\(n\) vector.

The decorrelated response and design matrix are then \[ y_t^* = F_t^{-1/2}\Bigl(y_t - \sum_{j \in N(t)} B_{t,j}\, y_j\Bigr), \qquad X_t^{(k)*} = F_t^{-1/2}\Bigl(X_t^{(k)} - \sum_{j \in N(t)} B_{t,j}\, X_j^{(k)}\Bigr), \tag{18}\] for \(k = 1,\ldots,K\). The entire NNGP construction requires \(O(nm^3)\) work (dominated by the \(n\) Cholesky solves of dimension at most \(m\)), and the decorrelation map requires \(O(nmK)\) work.

GLS Estimation of Mean Coefficients

For fixed kernel-shape parameters \(\boldsymbol{\eta}\) and covariance parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\), the mean is linear in \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\). Writing \(\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X}(\boldsymbol{\eta})\) for the \(n \times K\) design matrix and \(\mathbf{X}^*\), \(\mathbf{y}^*\) for their decorrelated counterparts, the GLS estimator reduces to ordinary least squares on the whitened data: \[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^{*\top}\mathbf{X}^*)^{-1}\mathbf{X}^{*\top}\mathbf{y}^*. \tag{19}\] This is equivalent to \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{y}\) with \(\mathbf{Q} = \widetilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^{-1}\), but the whitened form is computationally preferable because neither \(\mathbf{Q}\) nor \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\) need to be formed explicitly. The closed-form solution for \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) means that only the \(2K + 3\) nonlinear parameters \((\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\) require iterative optimization.

Nonlinear Optimization

The full parameter vector consists of \(2K\) kernel-shape parameters (two per kernel: \(\log\delta_k\) and \(\log\sigma_k\)) and three covariance parameters (\(\log\sigma^2\), \(\log\phi\), \(\log\tau^2\)), all on unconstrained log scales with bounded search regions. The objective is the negative GLS log-likelihood: \[ -\ell(\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = -\left[-\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=1}^{n}\log F_t - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{t=1}^{n}(y_t^* - \hat{y}_t^*)^2\right], \tag{20}\] where \(\hat{y}_t^*\) is the decorrelated predicted mean. For each candidate \((\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\), the objective is evaluated by:

  1. Building the design matrix \(\mathbf{X}(\boldsymbol{\eta})\) via kernel convolution.
  2. Constructing the NNGP factors \(\mathbf{B}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) and \(\mathbf{F}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\).
  3. Decorrelating \(\mathbf{y}\) and \(\mathbf{X}\) to obtain \(\mathbf{y}^*\) and \(\mathbf{X}^*\).
  4. Solving for \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) via Equation 19 and computing the residual sum of squares in the whitened space.

The optimizer is CMA-ES (Hansen and Ostermeier 2003), a derivative-free global search algorithm suited to the moderate dimensionality and multimodal geometry of the kernel/covariance parameter space. The optimization proceeds over the full \((\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\) vector jointly, with \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) profiled out at each evaluation. After convergence, the final \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) is obtained by GLS at the optimized \((\hat{\boldsymbol{\eta}}, \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})\).

Prediction and Kriging

Three prediction modes are available, corresponding to increasing use of temporal context:

Simulation (mean prediction). Given rainfall \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\), the design matrix is constructed from the fitted kernel parameters and the point prediction is \(\hat{y}_t = \mathbf{X}_t \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\). When a Box-Cox response transformation is used, the back-transformed point prediction is the conditional median \(\exp(\hat{\mu}_t) - \epsilon_0\), where \(\epsilon_0\) is the shift constant applied before transformation to ensure positivity. The conditional median is used rather than the lognormal mean \(\exp(\hat{\mu}_t + \hat{\sigma}_t^2/2)\) to avoid systematic positive bias.

History-aware simulation. For out-of-sample prediction, the test rainfall is concatenated with the training rainfall before convolution, ensuring that the lagged kernel structure has access to the full rainfall history at the train–test boundary. The test segment is then sliced from the concatenated predictions.

Kriging (conditional mean correction). Kriging exploits the estimated NNGP structure to correct the simulation prediction using observed residuals at neighboring time points: \[ \hat{y}_t^{\mathrm{krig}} = \hat{\mu}_t + \sum_{j \in N(t)} B_{t,j}\, \epsilon_j, \qquad \epsilon_j = y_j - \hat{\mu}_j, \tag{21}\] where \(\hat{\mu}_t\) is the mean prediction on the (possibly transformed) scale. This is the temporal analogue of Equation 12: the kriging coefficients \(\mathbf{b}_t\) from the NNGP provide an optimal linear correction for the residual at time \(t\) based on observed residuals at its \(m\) nearest predecessors. The conditional kriging variance is \(F_t\), the diagonal element of the NNGP conditional variance matrix, which provides the basis for prediction intervals.

Neural Network Comparator: NN-GP

To assess whether the SWR mean adequately captures the rainfall-streamflow relationship, the same GP covariance framework is paired with a neural network mean function, yielding a temporal adaptation of the NN-GLS algorithm from Section 3.

The input to the neural network at each time step is a lag vector \(\mathbf{z}_t = (x_t, x_{t-1}, \ldots, x_{t-L+1})^\top\) of dimension \(L\) (the lag window), where \(L\) plays the role of the maximum lag. The mean function is a two-hidden-layer MLP: \[ f_{\mathrm{NN}}(\mathbf{z}_t) = \mathbf{w}_3^\top\, g\bigl(\mathbf{W}_2\, g(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{z}_t)\bigr), \tag{22}\] where \(g(\cdot) = \mathrm{ReLU}(\cdot)\) is applied elementwise and \(\mathbf{W}_1, \mathbf{W}_2, \mathbf{w}_3\) are trainable weight matrices. The hidden dimension is a tunable capacity parameter.

Training follows the iterative NN-GLS scheme. At each GLS iteration:

  1. The NNGP matrices \(\mathbf{B}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) and \(\mathbf{F}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) are rebuilt from the current covariance estimate.
  2. The MLP is trained for a fixed budget of epochs by backpropagation on the decorrelated GLS loss: \[ \mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}\left[F_t^{-1/2}\Bigl(y_t - f_{\mathrm{NN}}(\mathbf{z}_t) - \sum_{j \in N(t)} B_{t,j}\bigl(y_j - f_{\mathrm{NN}}(\mathbf{z}_j)\bigr)\Bigr)\right]^2. \tag{23}\]
  3. The covariance parameters \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) are re-estimated from the updated residuals by NNGP marginal log-likelihood (MLE).

The total epoch budget is divided evenly across GLS iterations to maintain a fixed compute envelope. Gradient clipping, early stopping, and \(L_2\) regularization stabilize training, particularly under transformed responses where the decorrelated loss surface becomes ill-conditioned.

The NN-GP model serves a diagnostic role. If it substantially outperforms the SWR mean in held-out metrics, nonlinear rainfall-to-streamflow dynamics are present that the kernel convolution cannot capture. If SWR performs comparably, the linear structure is adequate and the more interpretable specification is preferred. Both models share the same GP covariance and NNGP machinery, so the comparison isolates mean-function flexibility.

Model Selection and Information Criteria

Within each model family, the number of kernels \(K\) (or NN capacity) is selected using an effective Bayesian information criterion that accounts for residual autocorrelation: \[ \mathrm{BIC}_{\mathrm{eff}} = -2\,\ell + p\,\log(n_{\mathrm{eff}}), \tag{24}\] where \(\ell\) is the GLS log-likelihood, \(p\) is the effective number of parameters (\(3K + 3\) for SWR-GP, or the total network parameter count plus three covariance parameters for NN-GP), and \(n_{\mathrm{eff}}\) is the effective sample size estimated from the residual autocorrelation structure. For NN-GP, within-model capacity selection uses held-out training-tail kriging CRPS to avoid information-criterion instability in overparameterized settings. Between-model comparison uses test kriging CRPS.

Theoretical Basis

The theoretical requirements for the temporal adaptation are the direct analogues of the spatial conditions in Section 3. Full column rank of \(\mathbf{X}(\boldsymbol{\eta})\) and positive definiteness of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) ensure identifiability of \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) for fixed \((\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\). The ordered-neighbor precision converges uniformly to the exact GP precision, following from the decay properties of the Matérn covariance, which implies asymptotic equivalence between the approximate and full GLS criteria. The finite-sample error rate for the NN comparator inherits the spectral-interval dependence on the discrepancy matrix from the Zhan and Datta framework. Formal statements and proofs are in the Appendix.

Empirical Validation

This section evaluates the temporal SWR-GP framework through three studies: a simulation experiment verifying parameter recovery under controlled conditions, a real-data benchmark on the Big Sur daily hydrological series, and calibration diagnostics assessing predictive distribution quality.

Data and Experimental Design

The evaluation uses the Big Sur daily hydrological series, spanning 40 hydrological years with October/September year boundaries. The first 29 years form the training set (\(n_{\text{train}} = 10{,}501\)) and the remaining 11 years the held-out test set (\(n_{\text{test}} = 3{,}652\)).

The benchmark compares five model variants across two axes: model family and response transformation. SWR uses a kernel-convolution mean with no explicit GP covariance; its corrected-prediction mode applies the Cochrane–Orcutt AR predictor from Section 2. SWR-GP pairs the same kernel-convolution mean with a temporal NNGP covariance and uses the kriging predictor from Equation 21. NN-GP replaces the kernel mean with a neural network while retaining the same NNGP covariance structure, serving the diagnostic role described in Section 4. SWR and SWR-GP are each fitted with both untransformed responses and Box-Cox (\(\lambda = 0.2\)) transformed responses (Box and Cox 1964), yielding four kernel-convolution variants. NN-GP is run with untransformed responses only: the neural network’s nonlinear activations can internally approximate monotone transformations, making an external Box-Cox layer redundant for a mean-function comparator. Throughout this section, “kriging mode” refers to the NNGP temporal kriging predictor for SWR-GP and NN-GP variants, and to the Cochrane–Orcutt AR-corrected predictor for SWR variants.

Each variant reports predictions in two modes: simulation (mean prediction only) and corrected prediction (mean plus residual correction). This factorial design isolates the separate effects of model family, response transformation, and prediction mode on held-out performance.

Common settings: Gaussian lag kernel, \(K \in \{1,2,3,4\}\) with complexity selected by \(\text{BIC}_{\text{eff}}\) for SWR and SWR-GP and by held-out training-tail kriging CRPS for NN-GP, \(m = 20\) nearest neighbors, \(\nu = 1.5\), and maximum lag 100 days. Performance is reported using NSE (Nash and Sutcliffe 1970), KGE (Gupta et al. 2009), CRPS (Gneiting and Raftery 2007), and RMSE. The primary comparison criterion is held-out test kriging CRPS. The benchmark uses \(m = 20\) neighbors rather than the \(m = 10\) used in the simulation study; the larger value better captures the longer effective dependence range of the Big Sur residuals.

Simulation Study for Parameter Recovery

Before applying the framework to real data, a simulation study assesses the finite-sample identifiability and recovery of SWR-GP parameters under controlled conditions. This is motivated by the theoretical identifiability results of Section 4 and by the practical concern that nonlinear kernel-shape optimization may fail to separate mean and covariance components in finite samples.

Synthetic streamflow series of length \(n = 2{,}500\) are generated from the SWR-GP data-generating process using the observed Big Sur rainfall record as the covariate input. The simulation grid crosses three levels of kernel complexity (\(K \in \{1,2,3\}\)) with three noise tiers (low: \(\tau^2 / \sigma^2 \approx 0.01\); medium: \(\tau^2 / \sigma^2 \approx 0.08\); high: \(\tau^2 / \sigma^2 \approx 0.3\)), yielding nine cells with five replications each. True kernel-shape parameters \((\delta_k, \sigma_k)\) are drawn with a minimum lag separation of 10 days to prevent aliasing, and true covariance parameters \((\sigma^2, \phi, \tau^2)\) are drawn from ranges representative of hydrological residual processes. The fitting procedure uses four random restarts of the CMA-ES optimizer at each cell to mitigate sensitivity to initialization.

Table 1 summarizes the recovery results averaged across replications, and Figure 1 displays the corresponding error profiles across noise tiers.

First, mean-function recovery (Mean RMSE column) is consistent across all cells: reconstruction errors remain below \(0.13\) in all but one cell (\(K{=}1\), medium noise, where a high-variance replication produces \(0.308\)). Figure 1 (left panel) confirms this visually: the error bars for \(K{=}2\) and \(K{=}3\) are tightly clustered, while the \(K{=}1\) medium-noise outlier is driven by a single replication that settles on a local optimum. Across the remaining eight cells, mean RMSE stays below \(0.13\), confirming that GLS estimation separates the SWR mean from the residual process.

Second, covariance parameter recovery is sensitive to the noise tier. In the low-noise cells, where the nugget is small relative to the signal variance, the optimizer struggles to distinguish \(\sigma^2\) from \(\tau^2\), producing \(\tau^2\) relative errors exceeding 1.0 at all three \(K\) values. This is expected: when \(\tau^2 \approx 0\), the likelihood surface along the \(\sigma^2\)/\(\tau^2\) ridge is flat, a well-known identifiability challenge in GP models. Figure 1 (right panel) shows this pattern: the \(K{=}1\) low-noise cell has a \(\sigma^2\) relative error near \(1.0\), whereas the same cell’s \(\tau^2\) error is inflated because the optimizer cannot separate the two variance components. At medium and high noise levels, \(\tau^2\) relative errors drop below \(0.04\).

Third, lag-shape recovery degrades with increasing \(K\): the mean lag relative error rises from \({\sim}0.09\)–\(0.13\) at \(K = 1\) to \({\sim}0.43\) at \(K = 2\) and \({\sim}0.55\) at \(K = 3\) (Figure 1, center panel). This is driven by kernel-label permutation and partial aliasing between closely spaced kernels, not by failure of the composite mean reconstruction. The optimizer recovers the correct \(\widetilde{y}_t\) while assigning individual kernel centers to permuted or shifted positions. This has limited consequence for prediction, since \(\widetilde{y}_t\) is what enters the likelihood and kriging predictor, but individual kernel parameters should be interpreted with caution when \(K > 1\).

The simulation study confirms that the SWR-GP mean/covariance decomposition is estimable in samples comparable to the Big Sur training set. Covariance recovery requires adequate nugget presence to resolve the variance partition, and the framework supports parameter interpretability under realistic conditions.

Benchmark Results and Model Comparison

Table 2 and Table 3 report the held-out test metrics for all five variants. The primary criterion, held-out test kriging CRPS, reveals a clear ordering. All five variants selected \(K = 4\) kernels under their respective complexity-selection rules, indicating that \(\text{BIC}_{\text{eff}}\) consistently favors the richer kernel decomposition at this data scale.

Figure 2 shows observed and predicted streamflow for SWR-BC over a representative two-year window from the held-out test period. The kriging prediction (blue) tracks the observed hydrograph closely through both peak events and recession limbs, while the simulation prediction (red) captures the overall shape but misses the timing and magnitude of individual peaks. The improvement from simulation to kriging is visually consistent with the \(41\%\) CRPS reduction.

Figure 3 displays the four fitted lag kernels for SWR-BC, weighted by their regression coefficients \(|\beta_k|\). Kernel \(k{=}2\) (\(\bar\ell = 0.2\)d) captures the immediate surface-runoff response; kernel \(k{=}3\) (\(\bar\ell = 3.1\)d) represents a short-delay interflow component; and kernel \(k{=}1\) (\(\bar\ell = 41\)d) captures a broad, slow baseflow contribution. Kernel \(k{=}4\) has a negative coefficient (\(\beta = -0.42\)) and a mean lag exceeding the display window, acting as a slow-varying seasonal correction. The decomposition illustrates the interpretability of the SWR mean function: each kernel corresponds to a physically plausible runoff pathway.

Response transformation. The Box-Cox transformation (\(\lambda = 0.2\)) is uniformly beneficial. In kriging mode, it reduces test CRPS by \(0.485\) for SWR (from \(0.794\) to \(0.309\)) and by \(0.427\) for SWR-GP (from \(0.799\) to \(0.372\)). Without Box-Cox, the untransformed variants produce kriging CRPS values near \(0.80\), more than twice the transformed counterparts. The Box-Cox compression of the upper tail brings the right-skewed streamflow distribution closer to the Gaussian assumption underlying both families.

Simulation versus kriging. The simulation-to-kriging improvement is large and consistent. For SWR-BC, kriging reduces test CRPS from \(0.526\) to \(0.309\) (\(41\%\)). For SWR-GP-BC, the reduction is from \(0.899\) to \(0.372\) (\(59\%\)). Both correction mechanisms capture persistent serial dependence that the mean function leaves unabsorbed. At this site, the residual correction contributes more to out-of-sample accuracy than the choice of mean-function family.

The per-\(K\) breakdown reinforces this finding (Table 4). For SWR-GP-BC, simulation-mode CRPS improves substantially with \(K\) (from \(1.203\) to \(0.899\)), but kriging-mode CRPS is nearly flat: it drops from \(0.443\) at \(K{=}1\) to \(0.372\) at \(K{=}4\), with most of the improvement between \(K{=}1\) and \(K{=}2\). The GP covariance absorbs the bulk of the residual structure regardless of mean-function complexity. By contrast, SWR-BC kriging CRPS responds more strongly to \(K\): from \(0.454\) at \(K{=}1\) to \(0.309\) at \(K{=}4\), a \(32\%\) reduction. The simpler AR correction does not absorb mean-function deficiencies as aggressively as the GP, so improvements in the SWR mean translate more directly to better corrected predictions.

SWR-BC versus SWR-GP-BC. SWR-BC achieves kriging CRPS \(0.309\), outperforming SWR-GP-BC (\(0.372\)) by \(0.063\) units at \(1/50\)th the compute cost (\(7.6\) vs. \(407.2\) CPU seconds). The SWR-GP model is theoretically better aligned with the assumptions of Section 4, since it jointly optimizes kernel-shape, mean, and covariance parameters through a single profile likelihood. However, joint optimization over the full \((\boldsymbol{\eta}, \boldsymbol{\theta})\) space is harder than the two-stage SWR procedure. SWR-GP simulation-mode performance (test CRPS \(0.899\), NSE \(0.602\), KGE \(0.547\)) is worse than the untransformed SWR baseline (NSE \(0.650\), KGE \(0.720\)), indicating that the joint optimizer finds kernel-shape parameters that are suboptimal for the mean function — worse not merely relative to the Box-Cox SWR, but worse than the plain untransformed baseline in simulation mode. The Cochrane–Orcutt AR correction applied to the better-fitted SWR mean then compensates for the residual dependence.

This result does not invalidate the SWR-GP framework. It indicates that the joint optimization objective is more demanding than the two-stage alternative. A dedicated estimation-mode study confirmed that alternating-block strategies (updating \(\boldsymbol{\eta}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) in separate L-BFGS-B phases) achieve numerically identical kriging performance to the joint CMA-ES approach, ruling out estimation mode as the source of the gap. The difference is therefore structural: the two-stage procedure benefits from a better-conditioned kernel-shape optimization unconstrained by the covariance penalty.

NN-GP results. NN-GP-N achieves simulation-mode test CRPS \(0.773\), the best simulation CRPS among the five variants, reflecting the network’s ability to capture nonlinear rainfall-runoff relationships beyond the kernel-convolution structure. This advantage reverses in kriging mode: NN-GP-N reaches kriging CRPS \(0.598\), worse than both SWR-GP-BC (\(0.372\)) and SWR-BC (\(0.309\)). The simulation-to-kriging improvement for NN-GP-N is \(0.175\) CRPS units (\(23\%\)), far smaller than the \(0.527\) units (\(59\%\)) achieved by SWR-GP-BC.

The training-period metrics (Table 5) expose the mechanism. NN-GP-N train kriging CRPS is \(0.174\), compared to test kriging CRPS of \(0.598\), a gap exceeding \(0.42\) units. This gap is absent in SWR and SWR-GP, where test kriging CRPS is actually lower than train kriging CRPS by up to \(0.06\) units, reflecting that the test period is somewhat easier than the training period. Against this baseline, the NN-GP reversal is even more striking. The neural network memorizes the training series, producing near-zero in-sample residuals (train krig RMSE \(= 0.17\) cfs). The NNGP then fits a covariance model to these artificially small residuals, rendering the kriging correction ineffective out of sample. This is consistent with the finite-sample analysis of Zhan and Datta (2024): when working covariance is estimated from overfitted residuals, the spectral interval of the discrepancy matrix \(\mathbf{E}\) widens, inflating the estimation error bound.

At this site and data scale, the SWR kernel-convolution provides sufficient mean-function flexibility for out-of-sample generalization. The additional capacity of the neural network does not translate into held-out benefit and instead introduces overfitting of the covariance layer.

Calibration and Coverage Diagnostics

Probability integral transform (PIT) histograms assess marginal calibration of the predictive distributions on the held-out test set. For a well-calibrated model, the PIT values should follow a Uniform\((0,1)\) distribution (PIT variance \(= 1/12 \approx 0.083\)). Figure 4 presents the three most informative diagnostics, illustrating the improvement from simulation to kriging mode and the miscalibration introduced by neural-network overfitting. Innovation QQ plots on the transformed scale are in Appendix B.

For SWR-BC in simulation mode (Figure 4 (a)), the PIT distribution is left-skewed (PIT mean \(0.40\), variance \(0.056\)), indicating that the model tends to overpredict. During extended dry periods, the mean prediction carries forward positive baseflow components while observed flows drop to near zero, pushing observations into the lower tail of the predictive distribution. The transition to kriging mode (Figure 4 (b)) largely eliminates this bias: PIT mean improves from \(0.40\) to \(0.48\), confirming that the AR correction adjusts the conditional mean toward the observation. The kriging PIT is sharply peaked around \(0.5\), with variance \(0.028\) — well below the uniform target of \(0.083\). This indicates overdispersed prediction intervals: the conditional predictive distribution is wider than necessary for the majority of observations. The overdispersion is concentrated in the low-flow regime that dominates the record, where both observed and predicted values cluster near the lower bound and the prediction uncertainty interval comfortably brackets the range. In the high-flow tail, the situation reverses: innovation QQ plots (Figure 6 in Appendix B) show that standardized residuals reach \(\pm 15\) where the Gaussian reference predicts \(\pm 4\), indicating that the prediction intervals are too narrow for extreme events. The combination of overdispersion in the bulk and underdispersion in the tails is characteristic of a Gaussian model applied to heavy-tailed data, even after Box-Cox transformation.

SWR-GP-BC kriging shows a similar peaked PIT (PIT mean \(0.45\), variance \(0.029\); Figure 7 in Appendix B). The PIT mean is further from \(0.50\) than SWR-BC’s \(0.48\), consistent with the larger mean-function bias identified in the simulation metrics. Despite the \(0.063\) CRPS gap between SWR-BC and SWR-GP-BC, both exhibit comparable calibration structure, confirming that the performance difference is driven by mean-function fit rather than by the distributional quality of the residual correction.

The NN-GP-N kriging PIT (Figure 4 (c)) is strikingly different: a bimodal distribution with mass concentrated near \(0\) and \(1\) (PIT variance \(0.124\), exceeding the uniform value). Observations frequently fall in the extreme tails of their predictive distributions, meaning the prediction intervals are systematically too narrow. The neural network’s near-zero in-sample residuals cause the NNGP to estimate a covariance with small marginal variance (\(\hat\sigma^2 = 0.48\), \(\hat\tau^2 = 0.007\)). Out of sample, where the mean prediction is biased, these narrow intervals fail to cover the actual observations. The result is both mean bias and variance underestimation — a compounding failure that the PIT captures clearly.

Discussion

Contributions and Relationship to the Original Framework

The primary contribution is to demonstrate that the NN-GLS framework of Zhan and Datta (2024) transfers coherently from the spatial to the temporal domain. The spatial nearest-neighbor DAG is replaced by the natural causal ordering of a time series, but the key structural advantages are preserved: separation of mean and dependence, GLS estimation, scalable NNGP inference, and kriging with uncertainty. The implementation changes are modest: ordered-predecessor neighbor sets replace spatial nearest neighbors, and the kernel-convolution SWR mean replaces the neural network mean.

The choice of a linear SWR mean rather than a neural network is itself informative. In the original NN-GLS paper, the neural network mean is the central innovation, motivated by complex nonlinear covariate effects in spatial regression. In the hydrological application, the relevant nonlinearity is in the lag structure, not the functional form: rainfall influences streamflow through delayed, attenuated pathways that the Gaussian kernel convolution captures with \(2K\) shape parameters. The NN-GP comparator confirms this. The neural network achieves better in-sample fit but worse out-of-sample generalization, indicating that the kernel-convolution structure provides an appropriate inductive bias. This aligns with Zhan and Datta (2024): when the mean function is overparameterized relative to the data, covariance estimation degrades and GLS efficiency gains are lost.

Estimation Strategy: Joint versus Two-Stage

A notable finding is that the two-stage SWR-BC procedure (fit mean, then correct residuals with an AR model) outperforms the joint SWR-GP-BC optimization on held-out data. This is surprising because the joint profile-likelihood approach, better aligned with Section 4, should in principle be at least as efficient.

The discrepancy likely reflects the difficulty of the joint optimization objective. The SWR-GP criterion simultaneously searches over \(2K\) kernel-shape parameters and 3 covariance parameters, all of which interact through the decorrelation map. The simulation study confirms that the optimizer reliably recovers the composite mean function but can settle on suboptimal configurations of individual kernel-shape parameters, particularly when kernels are closely spaced. In contrast, the two-stage SWR procedure separates the optimization into two lower-dimensional, better-conditioned problems: kernel-shape estimation under OLS, followed by AR estimation on the residuals.

Limitations

Several limitations should be noted.

Single-site evaluation. All empirical results are based on a single watershed (Big Sur). The relative ranking of SWR-BC, SWR-GP-BC, and NN-GP may differ in basins with stronger nonlinear dynamics, more pronounced non-stationarity, or different data scales. Multi-site validation is necessary before the conclusions can be generalized.

Fixed smoothness. The Mat'{e}rn smoothness is fixed at \(\nu = 1.5\) throughout. The peaked but centered PIT histograms for the best Box-Cox variants in kriging mode (Figure 4 (b) and Figure 7) suggest \(\nu = 1.5\) is adequate for this site, though the overdispersion indicates room for sharper uncertainty quantification. Profiling over \(\nu \in \{0.5, 1.5, 2.5\}\) or using a generalized Wendland covariance could be explored in multi-site settings where smoothness is less predictable.

Gaussian observation layer. The Box-Cox transformation addresses marginal non-Gaussianity, but the model still assumes Gaussian errors on the transformed scale. For extreme flows, a more flexible observation model (generalized extreme value margin, non-Gaussian state-space formulation) may be needed. The covariance-layer framework is compatible with such extensions, but the computational implications have not been explored.

NN-GP overfitting. The neural network exhibits clear overfitting, as diagnosed by the train-to-test kriging CRPS gap. This supports the conclusion that SWR provides sufficient mean-function flexibility, but also reflects implementation limitations: the epoch budget and absence of structured regularization (spectral normalization, dropout calibrated to effective sample size) may contribute. A more carefully tuned NN-GP could narrow the gap, though it would not change the finding that the SWR mean is adequate for this site.

Temporal stationarity. The Mat'{e}rn covariance assumes second-order stationarity of the residual process. Hydrological time series can exhibit non-stationary behavior due to seasonal effects, land-use change, or climate trends. The present framework could accommodate non-stationarity through time-varying covariance parameters or a deformation-based approach, but this has not been pursued.

Connections to the Broader Literature

The GLS approach to handling dependent errors has precedents. Cochrane and Orcutt (1949) introduced the iterative OLS/AR correction for linear models with autocorrelated errors, which remains the standard in the SWR baseline. The NN-GLS framework generalizes this by replacing the AR structure with a full GP covariance and extending the mean to neural network form. The temporal adaptation presented here occupies a middle ground: the mean is nonlinear in kernel-shape parameters but linear in coefficients, and the covariance is a GP rather than an AR model. GLS estimation and kriging follow directly from Zhan and Datta (2024).

The NNGP approximation (Datta et al. 2016) is central to computational feasibility. In the temporal setting, the ordered-predecessor construction is particularly clean because the natural time ordering eliminates the reference-set ordering required in the spatial case. For a Mat'{e}rn covariance with finite range, the \(m\) most recent predecessors capture the dominant conditional dependence, so approximation error is controlled by the ratio of \(m\) to the effective range \(\phi\).

Directions for Further Work

Several extensions follow naturally from the present analysis.

First, the framework should be evaluated across multiple basins and data regimes to assess the portability of the present findings. Basins with stronger nonlinear dynamics or shorter records would provide a more challenging test of the SWR-versus-NN comparison and of the NNGP approximation quality.

Second, the joint SWR-GP optimization merits a dedicated study. The two-stage procedure outperforms the joint profile likelihood here, and the mechanism — better-conditioned kernel-shape estimation unconstrained by the covariance penalty — is identified but not fully characterized. A systematic comparison of joint, alternating-block, and two-stage strategies across a range of basins and sample sizes would clarify when joint estimation is preferable.

Third, the simulation-recovery study should be expanded to a larger grid of signal-to-noise ratios, dependence ranges, and sample sizes, ideally including an assessment of coverage properties under the estimated (rather than true) covariance parameters.

Fourth, the observation layer should be extended beyond Gaussian assumptions. A hierarchical formulation with a non-Gaussian response distribution (gamma, generalized extreme value) and a latent GP would better model hydrological extremes while preserving the mean/covariance separation.

Finally, the NN-GP training loop already employs NNGP MLE for covariance updates. More frequent alternation between network-weight and covariance updates, or a finer learning-rate schedule for the covariance step, could reduce the covariance-layer overfitting visible in the train-to-test CRPS gap.

Appendix: Proofs of Theoretical Properties

This appendix collects proofs and derivations for the theoretical statements used in Section 4. Throughout, let \(\boldsymbol{\eta}\) denote the SWR kernel parameters, let \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) denote the linear mean coefficients, and let \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) denote the covariance parameters. Write \(\mu_n(\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\beta})=\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\boldsymbol{\beta}\) for the mean vector and let \(\Sigma_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) and \(\tilde\Sigma_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) denote the exact and ordered-neighbor approximate covariance matrices, respectively.

Consistency under Temporal NNGP Approximation (Theorem 1)

Consider the approximate criterion \[ \widetilde Q_n(\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\beta},\boldsymbol{\theta}) =\frac{1}{n}\bigl(\mathbf{y}_n-\mu_n(\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\beta})\bigr)^\top \widetilde\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \bigl(\mathbf{y}_n-\mu_n(\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\beta})\bigr). \] Assume: (i) the parameter space is compact; (ii) the true parameter \(\psi_0=(\boldsymbol{\eta}_0,\boldsymbol{\beta}_0,\boldsymbol{\theta}_0)\) lies in its interior; (iii) the map \(\psi \mapsto \mu_n(\psi)\) is continuous uniformly in \(n\); (iv) \(\Sigma_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) and \(\tilde\Sigma_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) are uniformly positive definite over the admissible parameter space; and (v) the ordered-neighbor approximation satisfies \(\sup_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\|\tilde\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})-\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\|\to 0\). Under these conditions, \[ \sup_{\psi}\bigl|\tilde Q_n(\psi)-Q_n(\psi)\bigr| \leq \frac{1}{n}\sup_{\psi}\|r_n(\psi)\|_2^2 \sup_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\|\tilde\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})-\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\| \longrightarrow 0, \] where \(r_n(\psi)=\mathbf{y}_n-\mu_n(\psi)\). Thus the approximate criterion converges uniformly to the exact GLS criterion. Standard M-estimation arguments then imply that any minimizer of \(\widetilde Q_n\) converges in probability to the unique minimizer of the population target.

The ordered-indexing assumption is what makes the approximation coherent in the temporal setting. Because each time point conditions only on preceding neighbors, the approximate precision admits the same triangular factorization logic as the spatial NNGP, but with temporal order replacing spatial graph order. The approximation error is controlled by how well the chosen neighbor depth \(m\) captures the effective dependence range of the residual process.

Identifiability of Mean and Covariance Parameters (Proposition 1)

Fix \((\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\theta})\) and define the GLS objective \[ Q_n(\boldsymbol{\beta}\mid \boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\theta}) =\bigl(\mathbf{y}_n-\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\boldsymbol{\beta}\bigr)^\top \Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \bigl(\mathbf{y}_n-\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\boldsymbol{\beta}\bigr). \] Differentiating with respect to \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) yields the normal equations \[ \mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})^\top\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\,\boldsymbol{\beta} =\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})^\top\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\mathbf{y}_n. \] Because \(\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\) is positive definite and \(\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\) has full column rank, the matrix \(\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})^\top\Sigma_n^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\) is positive definite. Hence it is invertible and the minimizer in \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) is unique, proving identifiability of the mean coefficients conditional on \((\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\theta})\). Changing \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) changes the weighting matrix but does not collapse column rank of \(\mathbf{X}_n(\boldsymbol{\eta})\); covariance reparameterization alters efficiency, not identifiability.

The broader separation claim follows from the modeling decomposition. The mean parameters enter through \(\mu_n(\boldsymbol{\eta},\boldsymbol{\beta})\) while the covariance parameters enter through \(\Sigma_n(\boldsymbol{\theta})\). Under joint identifiability of the mean and covariance families, equality of both induced distributions implies equality of the corresponding parameter values, so the decomposition is well posed. In practice, weak finite-sample confounding can arise when the mean is misspecified or the dependence range is short relative to the sampling interval, but this is a matter of numerical difficulty rather than non-identifiability.

Decorrelated GLS Equivalence (Theorem 2)

Take a factorization of the precision matrix \(\Sigma^{-1}=\mathbf{A}^\top\mathbf{A}\). Then \[ (\mathbf{Y}-\mathbf{f})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{Y}-\mathbf{f}) =(\mathbf{Y}-\mathbf{f})^\top\mathbf{A}^\top\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{Y}-\mathbf{f}) =\|\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}-\mathbf{A}\mathbf{f}\|_2^2. \] Defining \(\mathbf{y}^*=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{Y}\) and \(\mathbf{f}^*=\mathbf{A}\mathbf{f}\) gives the OLS-on-whitened-data identity used in the main text. In the ordered-neighbor setting, \(\mathbf{A}\) is induced by the sparse factors \((\mathbf{I}-\mathbf{B})\) and \(\mathbf{F}^{-1/2}\), so whitening is implemented sequentially. Since the whitening map built from the NNGP approximation converges uniformly to the one induced by the exact precision, the transformed criterion converges uniformly to the full GLS criterion. Optimization after decorrelation is therefore asymptotically equivalent to optimization under the exact covariance-weighted norm.

Finite-Sample Caveats

The preceding results should be interpreted with finite-sample caution. First, uniform approximation of the exact precision by the ordered-neighbor precision is an asymptotic statement; for fixed \(m\) there can be non-negligible approximation error when the effective dependence range is long. Second, nonlinear kernel-shape estimation induces a rugged objective surface, so asymptotic identifiability does not by itself guarantee easy optimization. Third, if the mean function is materially misspecified, covariance parameters may absorb signal that belongs to the mean, weakening finite-sample separation. These caveats do not invalidate the framework but explain why careful diagnostics and sensitivity checks remain necessary in practice.
